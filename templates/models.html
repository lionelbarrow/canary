Why does this work?

Investors buying US treasury bonds typically demand to be paid higher interest rates on long-term bonds than on short term-bonds. This reflects two things: the greater risk inherently associated with buying a longer-term asset, and the fact that holding money in the present is more valuable than holding it in the future.

Rarely, however, investors demand higher interest rates from short-term bonds than from long-term bonds. This behavior indicates that they believe that holding a small amount of money in the short term will be much more valuable than holding a larger amount of money in the long term. This can indicate that a recession is imminent. More generally, some economists believe that the distribution of these interest rates - generally referred to as the yield curve - can be used to forecast economic growth [4].

This regression takes advantage of this expectation by using the spread (difference) of the two rates as its main feature. However, interest rates can vary for reasons other than the general state of the economy, so the model also includes the rate at which banks lend each other money overnight, the Federal funds rate (FFR), to account for the interest rate environment.

How has this algorithm done historically?

The model is trained using historical data from January 1964 onwards. Before this point in US history, most long-term bonds were either callable or redeemable to pay estate taxes, making them structurally dissimilar to modern long-term bonds [1].

Since 1964 there have been 7 periods classified by the National Bureau of Economic Research (NEBR) as recessions. Note that the NEBR does not define a recession as two or more consecutive quarters of negative growth as is common in the media; a NEBR recession is the period of time between the peak of the business cycle and its trough.

If we start the algorithm at 1964 and play it forward, it successfully predicts 6 of the 7 NEBR recessions: it fails to predict the mild recession in the early 1990s but gets everything else. Additionally, it throws a single false positive: it predicts a recession in the late 60s that never happened. How are these numbers calculated?

The probabilities we display are output by a probit CDF regression using historical data, averaged monthly from 1964 to the present day. Two sources of data are used in the regression: the spread between the yields of 6-month and 10-year US Treasury notes, and the Federal funds rate. This model is mostly derived from a 2007 paper by Wright [1], with modifications inspired by more recent papers [2][3].
